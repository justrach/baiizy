Baiizy is a social concierge that helps you actually go out with friends, instead of just finding a cafe. You tell it what kind of hang you're in the mood for — deep work, late supper, low-pressure date, friend walk — and it answers with three real places nearby, a grounded reason each one fits your profile, a concrete opener you could paste into a text ("coffee at 10, leave by noon"), and the distance from every friend who might come along. It's for the kind of person who has four friends they'd love to grab lunch with but never actually pulls it together, because picking a venue is friction and matching availability is friction and proposing plans is friction.

The whole flow is built around real Singapore data through GrabMaps. You sign up with email and password, land in a four-step onboarding that captures your intents, social style, and free windows, and pin your neighborhood using GrabMaps' own autocomplete. On finish, we generate an OpenAI text-embedding-3-small vector of your entire profile and store it in Postgres with pgvector, indexed by HNSW for similarity-ranked friend suggestions. Then every recommendation, every friend match, every "who's free Saturday" query runs on real coordinates and real places, not mock data.

The Picks tab is where most of the product lives. A freeform search bar lets you type anything — "loud bar after work", "quiet coffee with Maya", "dessert walk at sunset" — and that phrase becomes the Grab keyword. Or you can tap an intent pill (Work, Lunch, Supper, Date, Friends) and let the agent choose keywords for you. Either way, GPT-5.4-nano picks three venues from the candidates and writes a grounded one-sentence "why it fits" plus a one-sentence "your move". End to end it returns in about four seconds. The prompt is tight, roughly four hundred tokens, with a Zod schema constraining the output, a thirty-second abort controller so it can never hang, and Vercel AI Gateway fallbacks to Claude Haiku and Gemini 2.5 Flash if the primary model stumbles.

Above the picks sits a dark Free Together panel that quietly does something nice. For every availability slot (weekday lunch, weekend afternoon, late night, and so on), we compute which friends of yours are free during that window and how many, show stacked avatars of the people who'd be in, and offer a one-tap "show picks" that filters the AI agent to that slot's natural intent. Weekend afternoons turn into friend-hang picks. Weekday evenings into supper picks. The algorithm uses real pgvector similarity for suggested friends in the first place, so the groups that cohere here tend to be groups that would actually show up.

And that's where the real algorithm shows itself. When you tap a Free Together slot, the recommendations endpoint pulls the live location of every friend who's free then, computes the centroid of the whole group, and runs the Grab search from that midpoint instead of just from you. Each recommendation card then displays per-friend distance chips: "you 0.9km · Maya 1.2km · Priya 1.5km · max 1.5km". The reasoning is visible. You can audit the math. A venue that's optimal for the group shows it. A venue that's nearest to you but far from two friends shows it. That transparency is what makes the group-optimization feel real instead of magic.

Distance-correctness mattered more than we expected. GrabMaps' POI search is relevance-ranked, not distance-ranked, so a "lunch" search near Holland Village will happily return Pepper Lunch Jurong Point fifteen kilometres away. We solved that with a hard three-kilometre haversine radius cap on candidates before they reach the LLM, plus a fallback that takes the two closest within ten kilometres if the tight radius is empty. The UI also surfaces which cached location drove the result — "Using live GPS, saved 2m ago, at 1.2917, 103.7929" — so you can see exactly why a given set of places came back.

Visually, we spent real time getting the map to feel like something worth opening on purpose. The Friends Map is a Zenly-style overlay where every friend is a circular avatar inside a colored ring, sitting directly on their live coordinate (not floating above a pin tail — the anchor is center, and we use a haversine breakdown on the selected card to prove it). The ring has a subtle two-and-a-half-second pulse animation, a soft drop shadow, and hover-scale on pointer-enter. Tap any marker or any friend card below the map and the camera does a 900-millisecond fly-to with a thirty-degree pitch, landing at zoom sixteen so you can actually see the block they're on. Zoom is deliberate: too wide and every avatar looks like it's on the wrong street; zoom sixteen makes the position believable. When all friends are visible, fitBounds caps at zoom 13.5 so the group frame stays tight instead of showing the entire island.

The map supports the usual keyboard shortcuts once it has focus — arrow keys pan, plus and minus zoom, shift-arrow rotates, and the canvas gets an amber focus ring so you can see where your input will land. A one-line hint below the map tells users this. Clicking anywhere on the map container focuses the canvas for you so you don't have to hunt for it.

Scattered across the rest of the product are the things that make it feel finished. The onboarding neighborhood input autocompletes against GrabMaps in real time and pins exact coordinates when you pick a result. Profile photos upload straight into a Cloudflare R2 bucket named grab-hack and serve back through our own authenticated proxy so the bucket stays private. Usernames validate to a clean a-z, 0-9, underscore format. A nearby-friend banner on the Picks tab pulses amber when any friend is within one kilometre of your live GPS, and a green "Tap in" button spins up a spontaneous event with the nearest cafe as venue, a fifteen-minute start time, and an invite to that friend — then routes you to /events/[id] so they see the RSVP immediately. Reviews live on every event's venue card with a five-star picker and averaging, and check-ins record what you did where.

Where we paid the most careful attention is graceful degradation. GrabMaps' style endpoint, vector tile endpoint, and font service each returned 502s at different points during the hackathon, and we documented fifteen concrete issues in bugs/grab-api.md. We snapshotted Grab's real "urban-light-partner" vector style while it was live — 123 layers, four sources — and route every asset (tiles, sprites, glyphs) through our own authenticated proxy with three-attempt exponential-backoff retries on 5xx. When Grab's font service is down, the proxy transparently substitutes Protomaps' Noto Sans glyphs with an x-baiizy-font-fallback response header, so labels keep rendering even during a partial Grab outage. When Grab's tile infrastructure is fully unreachable, we drop to a warm-tinted Carto Voyager raster style as a last resort, and flip the pitch to zero because raster tiles don't render under oblique projection. A /map3 page is a live demo of Grab's full vector style rendering end-to-end through our proxy, with no fallback logic at all, just to show what the 1:1 copy looks like. Throughout, a small amber pill tells the user "Not GrabMaps · using snapshot" when the fallback kicks in, so they're never confused about why the map looks slightly different.

Under the hood we're running Next.js 16 with Turbopack, React 19, TypeScript throughout, Tailwind v4, Drizzle ORM against PlanetScale Postgres, pgvector for embeddings, better-auth with Drizzle adapter for session management, Cloudflare R2 for uploads, Vercel AI Gateway for the LLM calls, MapLibre GL JS for rendering, and GrabMaps for everything geographic. Eleven API routes, fourteen pages, the whole thing hot-reloading in under three hundred milliseconds.

What we'd like judges to notice is the shape of the product. This isn't a places API wrapper. It's a tool for the specific moment when you realize you'd like to see a friend and don't know how to suggest it without making it weird. Every recommendation ends with a message you could actually send. Every group query shows its work. Every outage in the upstream is handled without the user seeing a broken page. The Free Together overlap, the centroid search, the per-friend distance chips, the avatar-centered markers at street-level zoom, the Protomaps font fallback — none of them are demos in isolation. They're a coherent answer to a single question: can we make going out with friends less heavy? The repo is at github.com/justrach/baiizy.
